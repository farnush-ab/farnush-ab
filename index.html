House in Two Plasters,
Lavasan.

The room you cannot photograph is the one you want most. A house in Lavasan, built into a north-facing hill at the end of a thirty-minute drive from the city; commissioned by a family that has lived in this district for three generations; drawn by an architect who refuses to discuss his work in interviews. We were asked to specify the lighting and the fixtures, not the furniture. We were given one rule: nothing should announce itself.

"The owner asked for two plasters. One for the day, one for the evening. We chose four lighting profiles to match."

The two plasters of the title are not metaphorical. The east-facing rooms received a cool grey lime; the west, a warmer ochre. Both finishes were laid by the same plasterer, who has worked on every house this architect has built since 1998. We worked backwards from the finishes: brass that read warm against the ochre and stayed honest against the grey; lighting at 2700K for the warm rooms and 3500K for the cool ones, with a single tunable line for the corridor between them.

The Vola FS1 was specified in three bathrooms — once in unlacquered brass, once in matte chrome, once in the architect's own custom satin finish (we wrote the request to Denmark; they answered in eleven days). A single Flos 2097/30 hangs over a thirty-six seat dining table on the lower floor, which we recommended at 30 bulbs over the standard 50, on the grounds that the room is already a chandelier without help.

The smart-home install is Lutron HomeWorks QSX with Crestron Horizon keypads — fourteen of them, each placed by hand by the architect, each labelled in a single typeface we agreed upon. The owner cannot tell you what brand the system is. He can tell you what time the corridor lights come up for breakfast.

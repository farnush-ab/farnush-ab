House in Two Plasters.

The brief, when it arrived, contained one sentence and one drawing. The sentence: "Two plasters, one house." The drawing: a section through a hill, with the kitchen at the lowest point and the bedroom at the top — and a single staircase between them, traced in pencil by what we later learned was the owner's daughter, aged seven. Three months later, the same staircase was poured in concrete and lit by a single profile of Erco Quintessence at 3000K, raked across the wall to catch the trowel marks.

"We do not light the staircase. We light the plaster."

This is the central pleasure of working in private residences at this scale: a client will not let you photograph the rooms, but they will let you spend an hour in front of a sample with the architect, arguing about whether to specify the Vola FS1 in unlacquered brass or in the architect's custom satin. We chose both — unlacquered on the master, satin on the guest. The unlacquered will tarnish; the satin will not. The architect prefers tarnish. The client prefers parity. We took the question, sent it to Denmark on a Wednesday, and had an answer by the following Monday.

The Flos 2097/30 above the dining table is specified at 30 bulbs out of a possible 50. We recommended this against the family's wishes. They had seen the fixture at the showroom — at 50 — and asked for it as photographed. We held the line. A chandelier of this density, hung at 3.2m over a six-metre table, becomes the dining room. With 30 bulbs, it becomes the architect's punctuation mark.

The cool plaster is the harder of the two to live in. Its temperature reveals every error. We chose the Vola in satin against this wall because the matte finish absorbs the daylight from the clerestory rather than throwing it back at the mirror. The wash on the wall remains the brightest object in the room — which, in a bath of this scale, is the only honest place to put attention.

Three weeks after handover, the architect sent us a photograph of the family's cat sitting in the Agape. The picture is on the wall of our Hub. We have not been allowed to publish it.
